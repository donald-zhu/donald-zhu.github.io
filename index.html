No.1 — Looking in Chinatown: A Pluralistic Urban Space

(Editorial, Ethnography, Installation, Publication)

The following blurb book pages document a project from its research stage to the final making response. For the intent of the work,
I conducted an ethnography observation in Chinatown, Toronto. Upon extensive investigation, it became clear that this ethnic enclave is a pluralistic looking space—one that involves many different kinds of looking behaviors by and at different demographics. As one photographs (to look with a camera) in this space, they are choosing one thing above all else to take photos of. And this “thing” in Chinatown—a place that amalgamates vastly different styles and influences—is often given a peculiar status depending on the subject’s expectations. Therefore, I made a prototype installation piece as a public intervention. It is a “looking piece” / spectacle that is meant to highlight such juxtaposition and the dynamics at play.

No.2 — Hito Steyerl's In Defense of the Poor Image (1/2)

(Web Design,
Media Studies)

This project is meant to agitate and understand the relationship between medium and content. Hito Steyerl's essay, In Defense of the Poor Image, is set in multiple different ways (web, installation, and book—two of which are showcased in this portfolio). By pushing the same substance through the shape of different formats, the agency of these mediums is made apparent. As Steyrel writes, the poor images better signify the ways information is distributed through the software medium.
Images, text, and information that are degraded by programs and codes are often left outside of the normative value hierarchy. In my website, by pushing and compressing images and typographical forms, it attempts to flatten the context and signification of the otherwise significant images. As these culturally significant images randomly appear on the screen, the viewer is given a curatorial opportunity to rearrange them in conscious and unconscious ways. And by happenstance, it could reveal new contexts and relationships that are normally ignored in these poor images.

No.3 — Get Type Right: Typography Manual

(Publication, Editorial,
Typography)

Get Type Right is a staple, fundamental publication about typography. It is a printed matter that is meant to act as a basic typography manual. The book is executed with close attention to typographic
details. The purpose of the project is to exercise and showcase my ability to tackle typesetting and editorial design with different typographical challenges (i.e. different measures, hierarchies, voices), as well as to demonstrate an understanding of typographical details.

No.4 — I Voted!

(Sticker for Instagram/
2019 Federal Election)

“I VOTED!” is a paid project commissioned by Instagram x OCAD U prior to the 2019 Candian election. It was a contest between 10 participants to design a story sticker to be used by Instagram users on election day. My design won second place in the competition. To provide message clarity, I employed simple quotations from the last Ontario election campaign: the “X”, the rounded
typography, and the non-partisan yellow used in the design. Simultaneously, its roundedness and playfulness also echo the identity of
Instagram. Moreover, I dedicated my work around the multilingual / culturalism in the Canadian settler state. With the careful use of semiotics, the “X” marks the individual civic act of voting. As it is the universal visual element across versions in different languages, voting itself is also a fundamental act that's shared by the vastly different citizens. It represents the important act of making one’s mark (on the ballot) and manifesting one’s presence on this land.

No.5 — Thrasher:
Construction/Deconstruction

(A Topological Investigation)

This Thrasher construction/deconstruction piece is a formal, taxonomic, and topological investigation into the information hierarchy present in printed publications. The Thrasher magazine is deconstructed into 7 respective categories. Through the vigorous “making as research”, fragments of the publication underwent different formal treatments and relations, in order to re-construct the visual voice of the magazine. After the rinse and repeat, this voice is then reconstructed through several 2D/4D mediums.

No.6 — Hito Steyerl's In Defense of the Poor Image (2/2)

(Book Design,
Publication)

This is part two of the typographical investigation of Hito Steyerel's In Defense of the Poor Image—printed and completed while in quarantine. This edition focuses more on the types and voices of poor images. Poor images, as a stand-in for undervalued carriers of significations in the hierarchical structure, have different connotations depending on the substance. Likewise, information, memories, and cultures are
circulated in the public sphere through different forms. And this book project is meant to highlight some of those forms—asserted, fragmented, retold, abstracted, plague-like, contemplative, ephemeral, digitized. This french-folded book also has inter-page displays of those treatments magnified.

No.7 — NO MORE LIES

(Branding/Intervention)

“NO MORE LIES” is a hypothetical music festival in Toronto that is meant to celebrate and galvanize an open exchange of experiences, as facilitated by music. Instead of using one top-down, imposed identity of the city, it invites for an idiosyncratic construction from the individuals that participate. Therefore this branding project is meant to situate the viewers within the space they encounter the posters (flags). By combining with transparent medium, the object helps to frame and locate the subject within the city, reverting the traditional
subject-spectacle relationship.
Furthermore, flags often are nationalist objects—a constructed identity. By taking that format and using it to represent individuals turns its signification on its head. On top of that, spontaneous and vague words related to the city and the land are put on the flag, as to provoke reactions from every person’s very subjective experiences.
